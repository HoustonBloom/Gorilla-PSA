01 — The Problem
Houston has a traffic problem.
More lanes won't fix it.

Houston is one of the most car-dependent cities in America. Near-universal reliance on driving, limited transit, and traffic that keeps getting worse every year. The city's response has been to build more lanes—but the data tells a different story.

The Katy Freeway: a lesson in what doesn't work

Houston widened the Katy Freeway to as many as 26 lanes—one of the widest highways on Earth. Within a few years, rush-hour travel times were worse than before. A 10% increase in highway capacity leads to roughly a 10% increase in driving volume, wiping out any relief.

The solution isn't more concrete. It's smarter driving at the bottlenecks that already exist.

One technique, adopted widely, could make a measurable difference—without spending a dollar.

02 — The Solution
The zipper merge.
See the difference.

When a lane is ending, you use both lanes all the way to the merge point, then take turns—one car from each lane, like the teeth of a zipper. Watch what happens compared to what most drivers do today.

Don't take our word for it — see the data ↓
03 — What Is a Gorilla PSA?
The city won't do this.
So we will.

Every state DOT that has reduced congestion with the zipper merge did it through public education campaigns—signage, PSAs, media outreach. Houston hasn't done any of that. No signs. No campaigns. No education. The city keeps adding lanes and hoping for the best.

A Gorilla PSA is citizens doing what their government won't. It's the education program that should already exist—run by regular people instead of institutions. You learn the zipper merge. You tell someone else. You call a radio station. You share this page. You do it again next week. That's it.

01

No budget needed

Governments spend millions on traffic campaigns. This costs nothing. Just people talking to people through channels that already exist.

02

Collective action

One person calling a radio station is a caller. Twenty people calling is a pattern. A hundred is a movement the city can't ignore.

03

Citizen outreach

Every conversation, every call-in, every share is someone doing the public education that Houston's transportation agencies have left on the table.

This is what grassroots looks like. No organization. No permission. Just Houstonians who got tired of waiting for someone else to fix it.

04 — Spread the Word
Spread the word.

The zipper merge works—but only if enough drivers do it. Right now, most Houstonians have never heard the phrase. That's the whole problem. You knowing isn't enough. You have to tell people. Here's what to say.

"Hey, have you heard of the zipper merge? When a lane is ending, you're supposed to use both lanes all the way to the merge point, then take turns—one car from each lane, like a zipper. Studies show it cuts backups by 40 to 50 percent. In Michigan they tried it and turned a 6-mile backup into 3 miles. AAA recommends it, DOTs across the country endorse it. We just gotta start doing it. Zipper merge—look it up."

06 — The Evidence
This isn't an opinion. It's data.
40–50%
Shorter traffic backups
The most widely validated figure in zipper merge research. Confirmed by transportation departments in Minnesota, Missouri, Colorado, and multiple independent studies.
Sources: MnDOT, MoDOT, CDOT, FHWA
6→3
Miles of backup cut in half
At a Michigan site, a 6-mile congestion zone was reduced to 3 miles. Drivers saved 15–25 minutes each.
Source: ITRE / NC State
54%
Travel time reduction on freeways
Microsimulation found zipper merging cut freeway travel times by 54% and reduced queue length by 87%.
Source: SEH Inc.
↓
Fewer crashes & less road rage
Equalizing speeds between lanes reduces rear-end and sideswipe crash risk. Less aggressive driving observed in field evaluations.
Sources: MnDOT, NCDOT, Texas A&M TTI
